The Goal

Remove from the board the pieces of your opponent, by making mills (aligned pieces).

  Rules

Two players compete on a board, on which 24 slots are drawn. Some slots are connected and are said to be adjacent slots.
The connected slots from three squares of 8 slots.
A straight line of 3 adjacent slots is a mill.
There are 16 mills: the 12 edges of the squares, and 4 horizontal or vertical lines connecting the centers of the edges.
The players play alternatively. BLACK plays first, then WHITE. Each player has a stock of 9 pieces.
The game has three phases.
  • Placement As long as you have pieces to put on the board (MY_STOCK is not zero), you must PUT a piece from your stock onto an empty slot on the board.
  • Movement When you have no more pieces to put on the board (MY_STOCK is zero), you must MOVE one of your pieces to an adjacent, empty slot.
  • Jumping When you have only three pieces left (MY_STOCK is zero and MY_PIECES is three), you must MOVE one of your pieces to any empty slot, regardless of adjacency. In other words, you may jump.
In any phase, when your move (or put) results in a mill newly filled with your pieces, you must remove from the board a piece of your opponent, that is not itself part of a filled mill.
A mill is not new when you have already filled it with the same pieces (), in any order.
During the placement phase, it is possible to fill two mills in one turn. In such a case, you must remove two pieces of your opponent.
The game is over when any of the following conditions occurs:
  • Each player has played 200 turns.
  • Some player looses.

You lose if:
  • You have only two pieces left (MY_PIECES is two, and MY_STOCK is zero).
  • Your program times out.
  • Your program provides invalid output for the active phase.
  • When the game is over, you have less pieces on the board than your opponent.
A tie is possible, should the game end when both players have the same number of pieces on the board.

  Game Input

Coordinates:
A slot on the board is identified with a DIRECTION and a RADIUS.
The DIRECTION identifies a direction from the center of the board: 0 for top left, 1 for top, 2 for top right, and son on.
The RADIUS identifies the square: 0 for innermost square, 1 for middle square, 2 for outermost square.
Input for one game turn:
First line: MY_COLOR TURNS_LEFT MY_PIECES OPPONENTS_PIECES MY_STOCK OPPONENTS_STOCK
  • MY_COLOR: either BLACK (first player) or WHITE (second player).
  • Integer TURNS_LEFT: the number of turns before game over (starts at 200).
  • Integer MY_PIECES: the number of your pieces on the board, which is also your score.
  • Integer OPPONENTS_PIECES: the number of your opponent's pieces on the board (his/her score).
  • Integer MY_STOCK: the number of pieces you still have to put on the board (0 after the placement phase).
  • Integer OPPONENTS_STOCK: the number of pieces your opponent still has to put on the board.
Next line: Integer NUM_PIECES: the total number of pieces on the board. At least 6.
Next NUM_PIECES lines: PIECE_ID OWNER_ID DIRECTION RADIUS
  • Integer PIECE_ID: a unique identifier for the piece, in the range 1 to 18.
  • Integer OWNER_ID: 0 for one of your pieces, 1 for one of your opponent"e;s pieces.
  • Integers DIRECTION and RADIUS: the coordinates of the piece on the board.
Next line: Integer NUM_OPTIONS: the total number of suggested moves. At least 1.
Next NUM_OPTIONS lines: a line that would be a valid output for this turn.
All valid outputs are given, except when NUM_PIECES is 3. In the latter case, jumps are allowed. This could result in a high number of possible moves. To spare bandwidth, only moves to adjacent slots are given. But, if no such moves exist, then all jumps are provided. It is safe to write a program that just picks up some provided option, but, for better results, your program should predict the jumps by itself.
Output
A single line containing A PUT or a MOVE, depending on the current phase.
Piece removal:
Output commands end with two numbers, specifying which opponent's pieces should be removed in case of mills.
If there is one newly filled mill, the piece with ID REMOVE_PIECE_ID_1 is removed, if this is possible.
It is possible if both the ID identifies one of your opponent's pieces on the board, and this piece is not part of a filled mill (or all the pieces of your opponent belongs to filled mills).
If the removal of this piece is not possible, then some other piece of your opponent is automatically removed.
If there is a second filled mill in the same turn, REMOVE_PIECE_ID_2 is removed, if this is possible. Else, another piece is automatically removed.
You may want to use 0 for one or both of theses parameters, to say: 'I am not interested. Do what you want!'
Input for the placement phase (when MY_STOCK is not zero):

PUT DIRECTION RADIUS REMOVE_PIECE_ID_1 REMOVE_PIECE_ID_2

Put a new piece at the specified direction and radius. If some mills are filled, remove the given pieces (if possible).

Example: PUT 6 2 12 0
Put a piece at on the bottom left (direction is 6) outter (radius is 2) slot.
If one mill is filled, remove piece with ID 12 if it is possible. If not remove any piece of the opponent.
If a second mill is filled, remove any piece of the opponent (0 means 'not specified').

Input for the movement and jump phases (when MY_STOCK is zero):

MOVE PIECE_ID DIRECTION RADIUS REMOVE_PIECE_ID_1 REMOVE_PIECE_ID_2

Move piece PIECE_ID at the specified direction and radius. If some mills are filled, remove the given pieces (if possible).

Example: MOVE 4 6 2 12 0
Move piece 4 to the bottom left (direction is 6) outter (radius is 2) slot.
If one mill is filled, remove piece with ID 12 if it is possible. If not remove any piece of the opponent.
If a second mill is filled, remove any piece of the opponent (0 means 'not specified').

Constraints
Response time for the first turn: lower than 1s
Response time per turn: lower than 50ms

Background

This game used to be popular in the Middle Ages in Europe. It has several names: Mills, Nine men's morris, marelles... See a description on Wikipedia.
